Evolutionary Genomics.

CUCKOO CO-EVOLUTION.

Cuckoos are locked in a coevolutionary arms race with their hosts, evolving egg mimicry and sex-specific color traits to evade detection. My research examines inheritance patterns of the genes facilitating this mimicry: revealing that maternal (mitochondrial and W-linked) genes largely control eggshell color, while other genes shape speckling. Female-limited traits—like gray vs. rufous plumage—are also linked to the female genome and appear to be maintained by balancing selection over millenia. In fact, plumage polymorphism has been maintained for so long that speciation has occured, and two species of cuckoo harbor the same genetic basis of this trait.

CROW EPIGENETICS.

In my research on Eurasian carrion, hooded, and hybrid crows, I investigated how epigenetic mechanisms like DNA methylation might contribute to evolution and speciation. Although such modifications are often proposed as flexible regulators of gene expression, I found that most methylation differences closely tracked underlying genetic variation rather than acting independently. Together with labmates, I also studied how birds with female-specific sex chromosomes (ZW system) handle gene dosage imbalance. In crows, females partially compensate by increasing chromatin accessibility along the single-copy Z chromosome, enhancing gene expression. DNA methylation varied by tissue, but did not appear to drive this regulation.

POLYPLOID CROP DOMESTICATION.

I am currently investigating the domestication history of breadfruit and guava—two tropical crop species with complex polyploid genomes—using long-read sequencing technologies. These approaches allow me to phase haplotypes, resolve structural variation, and trace ancestral contributions, providing new insights into how domestication shaped their genomic architecture.
